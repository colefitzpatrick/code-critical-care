# 05 · Clinical Reasoning — "where you'll see this"

This is the file that makes you a *nurse*, not a test-taker. For each case, **say the
reasoning out loud** before reading the walkthrough. The skill: hear a cue → recall the
mechanism → predict the assessment/action. That is exactly how the NCLEX clinical-
judgment model and a real ICU shift both work.

Format: **Cue → Mechanism (the why) → What you'd assess/anticipate.**

---

### Case 1 — "K⁺ is 6.8, patient on dialysis missed a session"
- **Say it first.** What's the mechanism, what do you do?
- **Reasoning:** K⁺ sets RMP (Module chain 4). High extracellular K⁺ → unstable cardiac
  membrane.
- **You anticipate/assess:** put them on a **cardiac monitor**, get an **ECG** (peaked
  T waves?), notify provider now, anticipate orders (calcium to stabilize membrane,
  insulin+glucose/albuterol to shift K⁺ in, then removal). You connected a lab number to
  the heart *through a mechanism* — that's the whole skill.

### Case 2 — Post-op patient, BP 78/40, HR 128, cool clammy skin
- **Reasoning:** baroreflex (negative-feedback loop) sensed low pressure → effector =
  ↑HR + vasoconstriction (clammy, cool). The tachycardia *is* the loop working.
- **You anticipate:** this is **compensation**, not stability — a working loop fighting a
  big disturbance. Compensated now can decompensate fast. Trend it, find the cause
  (bleeding?), escalate. *Compensatory ≠ fine.*

### Case 3 — Two patients, both temp 39.4 °C
- Patient A: post-op day 2, shivering, says "I'm freezing." 
- Patient B: marathon runner, hot dry skin, confused, found collapsed in heat.
- **Reasoning:** A = **fever** (set point raised; body defending it → shivering). B =
  **hyperthermia** (set point normal; heat-dissipation effector failed).
- **You do differently:** A → antipyretic + find infection source (treat the set point);
  B → **active external cooling** now (antipyretics won't help — the integrator is
  fine). *Same number, opposite logic, opposite action.* Most students miss this.

### Case 4 — Running D5W wide open; later: confusion, headache
- **Reasoning:** dextrose is metabolized → you're left infusing free water → relatively
  **hypotonic** → water shifts into cells → **cerebral edema**.
- **You anticipate/assess:** neuro checks, slow/stop the fluid, escalate. The infusion
  decision and the symptom are the *same osmosis chain* from `03`.

### Case 5 — Lactate rising, BP drifting down despite fluids
- **Reasoning:** hypoperfusion → cells low on ATP → Na/K pump failing → anaerobic
  metabolism (lactate) and cell swelling; also a **positive-feedback shock spiral**
  (↓perfusion → worse pump → ↓perfusion).
- **You anticipate:** this won't self-correct (positive feedback) — source control +
  aggressive escalation. Rising lactate = cells are losing the ATP/pump fight.

## ⭐ The detection map — one failed pump, five signals

You can't see a cell. You *can* see what its failure does to the chart, the monitor, and
the body. This is how a nurse "sees" cellular physiology:

| Pump (ATP-starved) fails → | What happens | Where you SEE it |
|---|---|---|
| K⁺ leaves cells | blood K⁺ ↑ | **LAB:** hyperkalemia · **MONITOR:** ECG peaked T waves |
| anaerobic metabolism | lactate ↑, blood acidic | **LAB:** high lactate, low pH (blood gas) |
| effective blood volume ↓ → kidney under-perfused | urine ↓ | **ASSESSMENT:** oliguria (little/no urine) |
| low blood flow | CO₂ not carried to lungs | **MONITOR:** EtCO₂ ↓ on the vent |
| capillary leak + resuscitation fluid | interstitial fluid ↑ | **ASSESSMENT:** visible/pitting edema, weight gain |

Note: Na⁺ floods *into* cells too, but blood sodium is governed by water balance, so —
unlike K⁺ — you do **not** read the Na⁺ shift off a lab. Cellular swelling is microscopic
(invisible); the swelling you *see* is **interstitial** edema (a different compartment).

> Say this in an interview: "One ATP-starved pump produces hyperkalemia, lactic
> acidosis, oliguria, a falling EtCO₂, and edema — so I can track a cellular problem from
> the bedside." That sentence is clinical reasoning.

## The transferable move
Every case was the same three steps:
1. **Cue** (a number, a vital, an order)
2. **Mechanism** — *which gradient/pump/loop is involved?* (this is your engineering edge)
3. **Prediction** — what to assess, anticipate, escalate

Practice by covering the "Reasoning/anticipate" lines and running steps 1→3 aloud. When
you can do all 5 smoothly, check the **Clinical** box in the Master Index.
