**Set A**
- A1. To stay alive by staying *different* from its environment — it spends ATP to
  maintain gradients.
- A2. 3 Na⁺ out / 2 K⁺ in, per 1 ATP; it moves both ions *against* their gradients
  (uphill), so it requires energy by definition (ΔG > 0).
- A3. "Does it require ATP / go against the gradient?" Down-gradient & free = passive;
  up-gradient & energy-using = active.
- A4. Simple diffusion (O₂, CO₂); osmosis (H₂O via aquaporins); facilitated diffusion
  (glucose via GLUT).
- A5. O₂ is small and nonpolar → dissolves through the lipid bilayer. K⁺ is charged →
  the hydrophobic core blocks it; it needs a specific channel = a point of control.
- A6. A pump performing work to move mass against a potential gradient, powered by
  hydrolysis of ATP (ΔG_pump > 0, paid for by ΔG_ATP < 0).

**Set B**
- B1. ≈ −70 mV; the **K⁺** gradient dominates RMP.
- B2. ↑ serum K⁺ → smaller K⁺ gradient → RMP rises toward threshold → transient
  hyperexcitability then inexcitability → **peaked T → wide QRS → sine wave → arrest**.
- B3. Low external K⁺ → larger gradient → cell hyperpolarized & electrically unstable →
  U waves, ectopy, lethal arrhythmias (and digoxin toxicity risk).
- B4. "K⁺ sets resting membrane potential, so 6.9 means the heart's electrical
  stability is failing — this needs a monitor and a provider now."
- B5. No ATP → Na/K pump stops → Na⁺ (and water) leak in unopposed, K⁺ leaks out →
  cell depolarizes and swells → membrane/organelle damage → cell death.

**Set C**
- C1. Toward higher solute concentration; driving force = difference in water's chemical
  potential (solute lowers it, water moves to equalize).
- C2. Cells swell (water enters); danger = cerebral edema / RBC lysis if rapid.
- C3. To pull water *out* of swollen cells — e.g., shrink a swollen brain
  (↑ICP). It osmotically dehydrates cells.
- C4. Stimulus → **Sensor** → **Integrator (set point)** → **Effector** → response →
  (feeds back to reduce the original change).
- C5. Fever = **set point** raised (integrator), body defends new temp → give
  antipyretic (reset the point). Hyperthermia = **effector/dissipation** failure, set
  point normal → externally cool. Same temperature, opposite mechanism → opposite action.
- C6. e.g., shock: ↓BP → tissue/cardiac ischemia → weaker pump → ↓BP further. It
  amplifies itself, so it won't reverse without an outside intervention.
