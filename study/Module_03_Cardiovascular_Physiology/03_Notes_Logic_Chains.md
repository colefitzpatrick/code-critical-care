# 03 · Notes as Logic Chains

*Because X → therefore Y → so the nurse does Z.* Bold terms → `_GLOSSARY.md`.

## Chain 1 — MAP is built, not magic
Venous return fills the ventricle → **preload** (stretch) → **Frank–Starling**: more
stretch = stronger contraction → **stroke volume (SV)**
**→** CO = HR × SV; **MAP = CO × SVR**
**→ nurse:** a low BP is *always* a low-CO or low-SVR problem. Ask "is the tank low
(preload), the pump weak (contractility), or the pipes wide open (SVR)?" — that triage
*is* shock classification.

## Chain 2 — The 3 dials of stroke volume
**Preload** ↑ → SV ↑ (Starling). **Contractility** ↑ → SV ↑. **Afterload** ↑ → SV ↓
(harder to eject), worse if the pump is weak.
**→ nurse:** fluids raise preload; **inotropes** raise contractility; **vasodilators**
cut afterload; **vasopressors** raise SVR. Every cardiac drip pulls one of these levers.

## Chain 3 — Why a very fast heart can DROP output
HR ↑ → less time in **diastole** → less filling → ↓ preload → ↓ SV
**→** beyond a point, CO = HR × SV *falls* despite the high rate
**→ nurse:** a rapid arrhythmia with falling BP is an emergency — rate control/cardiovert,
don't assume "fast = good output."

## Chain 4 — Coronary perfusion is a diastolic event
The heart muscle gets its own blood mostly during **diastole** (squeezed shut in systole)
**→** fast HR (short diastole) or low diastolic pressure → the pump starves itself →
ischemia (Module 01 Chain 1, in the heart)
**→ nurse:** tachycardia + hypotension in a cardiac patient = the heart eating itself;
escalate. (This is why we don't love HR 150 with BP 80/40.)

## Chain 5 — The baroreflex (link Module 01 Ch6)
↓MAP → **baroreceptors** → ↑HR, ↑SVR, ↑contractility → MAP restored (neg. feedback)
**→** so early shock can show a *normal* BP with tachycardia — the loop is hiding it
**→ nurse:** treat the tachycardia/narrowing pulse pressure as the early warning;
"normal BP" is not "fine." Compensation ≠ stable.

## Chain 6 — Ejection fraction & the failing pump (bridge to Module 04)
**EF** = SV / end-diastolic volume (fraction ejected). Low EF = systolic pump failure
**→** weak pump → ↓SV → body compensates (↑HR, fluid retention, vasoconstriction) →
those add load to an already-failing pump → congestion
**→ nurse:** the compensations become the problem — management *unloads* the pump
(↓preload with diuretics, ↓afterload with vasodilators). Full detail in Module 04.

## Chain 7 — Sepsis through the equations (link Modules 01–02)
Infection → vasodilation → **SVR ↓** → MAP = CO × SVR falls *directly*; capillary leak →
↓preload → ↓SV too
**→ nurse:** fluids refill preload; **vasopressors (norepinephrine)** restore SVR to
hold **MAP ≥ 65** — now you know *why* those are the septic-shock actions, not just
*that* they are.

---
### Compression (20 s)
> CO = HR×SV, MAP = CO×SVR. SV runs on preload/contractility/afterload. The heart feeds
> itself in diastole. The baroreflex hides early shock. Every BP problem is a CO or SVR
> problem — and the fix targets the exact broken term.
